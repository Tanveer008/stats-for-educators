## 🟢 EPISODE 01 POST

---

**Your class average is 62%. Is that good or bad?**

Trick question. You cannot know from the average alone.

Here is what I mean 👇

Last week I looked at two imaginary classes:
- Class A average: 65%
- Class B average: 65%

Same number. But when I plotted the data:

**Class A:** Most students scored between 60–70%. Consistent, clustered together.

**Class B:** Half scored below 50%. Half scored above 80%. Almost no one in the middle.

Same average. Completely different classrooms.

Class A needs one teaching approach.
Class B needs **two** — because it has two separate groups of students hiding inside one number.

The statistic that reveals this is called **Standard Deviation.**

It tells you how *spread out* your students are.

→ Low SD = your class is uniform → one lesson plan can work
→ High SD = your class is divided → you need differentiated instruction

I built a simple Python script that calculates this for any class dataset in under 10 lines of code. It even tells you what the number means in plain English.

## 🟡 EPISODE 02 POST

---

**"My students are doing better." — But are they, really?**

This is one of the most common things teachers say after a new teaching method.

But *feeling* like students improved and *knowing* they improved are two different things.

Here's a real scenario:

A teacher tries a new approach for 4 weeks.
Pre-test average: 58%
Post-test average: 67%

That's a 9-point jump. Looks great. But is it statistically meaningful — or just random variation?

This is where the **Paired t-test** comes in.

It compares the same students' scores before and after — accounting for natural score variation — and tells you whether the improvement is **real or noise.**

I ran the numbers on a sample dataset:

```
t-statistic: 4.23
p-value: 0.002
```

p < 0.05 → The improvement is statistically significant. ✅
The teaching method likely made a real difference.

You don't need to understand the math deeply.
You just need to know: **"Is my p-value below 0.05? Then the change is real."**

**I taught the same topic to 3 different classes using 3 different methods.**

Class A: Traditional lecture
Class B: CPA approach (Concrete → Pictorial → Abstract)
Class C: Peer teaching

The results were not what I expected.

One method outperformed the others — but only for students in a specific score range.

To figure this out properly, I used **ANOVA** (Analysis of Variance) — a statistical test that compares more than two groups at once.

The insight it gave me changed how I plan lessons.
